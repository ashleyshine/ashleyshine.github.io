Where Did My Time Go? Fall 2017 Edition

December 2017 Â· 17 minute read

All the code for this project is available on github.

‘Twas the night (ten days) before Christmas… when I finished fall quarter finals, came home for Winter Break, looked at my calendar, and realized something crazy - I had, for once, free time! To do whatever I wanted! Imagine that. Then I realized I also had something else: because I time-block with my calendar (Google Calendar to be specific) in order to plan and protect my time, I had (unknowingly) generated a detailed dataset on how I’ve spent my time in over the past year or two.

So I got to thinking - I bet I could pull that data off Google Calendar, wrangle it into a usable format, load it into a dataframe, and find some pretty cool trends in there! Aaaaaand this here is the brainchild of that idea.

Some notes:

  • If you’re wondering how accurate the data is, since it isn’t really possible to perfectly predict how long events will run / how much time needs to be dedicated to a given event - the answer is, to a degree, pretty accurate. I usually plan out my time blocks the day before and adjust them accordingly if I need more time or an event runs long. It helps me keep track of how I’ve been spending my time and figure out if I have enough time to /get things done/.
  • I’ve kept the data local on my computer, since some of it involves other people / details I don’t necessarily want to divulge. But instructions on how to get calendar data from gcal (and a script to help do it) can be found in this repo.
  • This is just a project for fun / learning purposes that gave me a chance to familiarize myself with some of python’s core data science modules.

Trends:

  • I started off the quarter mostly swimming for my workouts.
  • The frequency of my swims decreased as the frequency of my runs increased. There are a few weeks where I was switching off between the two to make sure I didn’t injure myself running again.
  • After week 5, I only ran for my workouts. At this point, I felt strong enough to just run with rest days. It was also getting cold enough in the evenings that the appeal of going for a run was far greater than the not-so-great appeal of going for a swim.

This area plot is a pretty cool way to visualize my time - the two categories on the bottom, studying and working, more or less make up the “base” of my time. When things get busy, they’re the things I still have to do no matter what. Everything else - mock trial and running/swimming are just icing on the cake. I’m pretty happy to see that there’s always a thin layer of working out & socializing on top of everything, even when the quarter really gets rolling.

It’s interesting that the time I spent cumulatively on studying/working/socializing/working out isn’t constant from week to week. I expected to see patterns like studying going down when socializing went up - in other words, I’d be compensating for time spent on one activity with another activity. Instead, what we’re seeing here is that the time spent on each of these activities moves up and down with other activities. For instance, in week 8, time spent on studying, working, and socializing all decrease together. (Honestly, I was probably in recharge mode after weeks 5 - 7).
